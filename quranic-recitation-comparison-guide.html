Maqam Analysis — What to Look For
▼

Common maqamat used in Qur'anic recitation:

Bayati Rast Hijaz Saba Nahawand Jiharkah Ajam

Pitch Histogram

Export the pitch track as CSV (right-click layer → Export). Plot the frequency distribution — peaks reveal the tonic and dominant scale degrees of the maqam in use.

Microtonal Inflection

Bayati and Saba use a ♭3 roughly 150 cents above the tonic — between Western E♭ and E. Look for pitch tracks hovering in this ambiguous zone rather than snapping to equal temperament.

Cadential Formulas

Watch the pitch track at the end of each ayah. Most reciters return to the tonic or a characteristic cadential pitch. Compare how different reciters handle the same cadence.

Maqam Modulation

Skilled reciters shift between maqamat within a single recitation. Look for sudden changes in the overall pitch range or characteristic intervals — especially at emotional or semantic peaks in the text.

7
Reciter Comparison — What to Look For
▼

Tempo & Duration

Compare how long each reciter takes for the same ayah. Hafs reciters (like Mishary) often differ markedly in pacing from Warsh reciters. Use the timeline ruler to measure.

Madd (Lengthening)

Elongated vowels show as sustained horizontal bands in the pitch track. Compare their duration across reciters — tajweed rules specify minimums, but reciters vary considerably above that.

Ornamentation

Look for rapid pitch oscillations (melisma / zahrafa). In the spectrogram these appear as shimmering vertical bands; in the pitch track as fast zig-zag curves. Al-Minshawi is known for pronounced ornamentation.

Tonal Center

Even reciters using the same maqam may set the tonic at different pitches. Note the absolute Hz value of each reciter's tonic — this reflects vocal range and regional convention.

Good reference: Kristina Nelson's The Art of Reciting the Qur'an (1985) remains the most thorough musicological study of recitation styles and is a useful companion to this analysis.
